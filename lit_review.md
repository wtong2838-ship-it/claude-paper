# Real options models for AI compute infrastructure investment

**No single published model combines all six features — real options under uncertainty, default risk, oligopoly competition, uncertain adoption timing, diminishing returns, and two-sided R&D/production allocation — needed to fully capture the AI compute investment problem.** The closest papers combine four of the six. This gap means a unified model would represent a genuine contribution to the literature. The building blocks, however, are remarkably well-developed: over 50 papers across top journals provide the theoretical infrastructure for each ingredient, and several papers at the intersection of two or three features are directly applicable to AI data center investment. Below, 40+ papers are organized into six thematic clusters, with emphasis on papers combining multiple features and their mapping to AI compute.

---

## 1. Foundational real options for irreversible capacity investment

The theoretical core of the AI compute investment problem rests on the real options literature pioneered in the 1980s. These papers establish that when investment is irreversible and uncertain, the option value of waiting creates a substantial wedge above the naive NPV = 0 threshold.

**McDonald, Robert and Daniel Siegel (1986), "The Value of Waiting to Invest," *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 101(4), 707–727.** The canonical model of optimal timing for an irreversible project whose value follows geometric Brownian motion. For reasonable parameters, firms should wait until benefits are roughly **twice investment cost** before committing. Directly frames the "invest now vs. wait" decision facing GPU data center builders. *Features: real options, demand uncertainty.*

**Pindyck, Robert S. (1988), "Irreversible Investment, Capacity Choice, and the Value of the Firm," *American Economic Review*, 78(5), 969–985.** Extends real options to *incremental* capacity choice — each marginal unit of capacity is valued as a separate option. The firm's value equals installed capacity plus options to add more. Under moderate uncertainty, optimal capacity is much smaller than under reversibility, and a large fraction of firm value consists of growth options. This maps directly to phased GPU cluster additions: how many racks to install today, given that each unit is partially irreversible but demand keeps evolving. *Features: real options, demand uncertainty, capacity choice, diminishing returns (via downward-sloping demand).*

**Majd, Saman and Robert S. Pindyck (1987), "Time to Build, Option Value, and Investment Decisions," *Journal of Financial Economics*, 18(1), 7–27.** Models sequential investment where projects take time to complete. Each expenditure buys an option on the next stage — the investment program is a compound option. The optimal policy is "bang-bang": invest at maximum rate or stop entirely. **Extremely relevant** to data centers requiring 2–4 years from planning to operation. Explains why firms might halt construction midway if demand signals deteriorate, and why longer build times increase the value of phased investment. *Features: real options, demand uncertainty, time-to-build.*

**Bar-Ilan, Avner and William C. Strange (1996), "Investment Lags," *American Economic Review*, 86(3), 610–622.** Shows that with sufficiently long construction lags, the standard relationship between uncertainty and investment can *reverse* — more uncertainty leads to **more** investment because firms must commit early or risk missing demand entirely. The 18–36 month lead time for data centers and GPU procurement makes this reversal plausible: firms bet early precisely because they cannot wait for confirmation. *Features: real options, demand uncertainty, investment lags.* The companion paper **Bar-Ilan and Strange (1999), "The Timing and Intensity of Investment," *Journal of Macroeconomics*, 21(1), 57–77**, extends this to show that uncertainty delays lumpy investment but increases its *size* when it does occur — potentially explaining why hyperscalers build megascale 100MW+ campuses rather than smaller incremental facilities.

**Bernanke, Ben S. (1983), "Irreversibility, Uncertainty, and Cyclical Investment," *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 98(1), 85–106.** Introduces the "bad news principle": under irreversibility, the investment decision is driven primarily by worst-case outcomes, not expected values. Firms delay to learn whether downside scenarios will materialize. For AI infrastructure, this implies firms should focus on the probability that enterprise AI adoption is slow or fails — the upside takes care of itself. *Features: real options, demand uncertainty with information resolution.*

**Brennan, Michael J. and Eduardo S. Schwartz (1985), "Evaluating Natural Resource Investments," *Journal of Business*, 58(2), 135–157.** Pioneered contingent-claims valuation of real assets, treating a copper mine as a portfolio of options (open, close, mothball, abandon). The operating-mode-switching framework maps well to data centers that can run at full capacity, idle partially, or be repurposed. *Features: real options, operating flexibility, stochastic price uncertainty.*

**Pindyck, Robert S. (1991), "Irreversibility, Uncertainty, and Investment," *Journal of Economic Literature*, 29(3), 1110–1148** and **Dixit, Avinash K. and Robert S. Pindyck (1994), *Investment under Uncertainty*, Princeton University Press** provide the authoritative survey and textbook treatment, respectively. The book's Chapter 9 covers competition, Chapter 11 covers incremental capacity choice, and both are essential references for any real options analysis of AI infrastructure.

## 2. Real options games and strategic oligopoly investment

This is the most directly applicable cluster. With 3–4 hyperscalers (Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta) making simultaneous irreversible bets on GPU capacity, the game-theoretic real options literature provides the sharpest available tools.

**Grenadier, Steven R. (2002), "Option Exercise Games: An Application to the Equilibrium Investment Strategies of Firms," *Review of Financial Studies*, 15(3), 691–721.** The seminal paper on equilibrium real option exercise under Cournot-Nash competition. Competition **drastically erodes the option value of waiting** — firms invest at near-zero NPV thresholds instead of the large positive thresholds predicted by single-firm models. A "myopic optimality" result makes the framework tractable: the Nash equilibrium exercise strategy can be derived as if determined by a myopic monopolist in a transformed problem. Directly explains the AI arms race where competitive pressure forces aggressive investment despite high uncertainty. *Features: real options, Cournot-Nash oligopoly, demand uncertainty.*

**Grenadier, Steven R. (1996), "The Strategic Exercise of Options: Development Cascades and Overbuilding in Real Estate Markets," *Journal of Finance*, 51(5), 1653–1679.** Shows that rational strategic option exercise produces "development cascades" — concentrated bursts of investment that can occur even during declining demand, endogenously generating boom-bust cycles. The overbuilding mechanism maps precisely to AI data center concerns: once one hyperscaler commits, others rush to preempt, creating aggregate excess capacity even though each individual decision is rational. *Features: real options, duopoly, demand uncertainty, overbuilding dynamics.*

**Huisman, Kuno J.M. and Peter M. Kort (2015), "Strategic Capacity Investment Under Uncertainty," *RAND Journal of Economics*, 46(2), 376–408.** Perhaps the **closest single paper to the AI compute problem**. Firms choose *both* investment timing and capacity level in a duopoly under demand uncertainty. The first mover overinvests to deter the follower; under high uncertainty, the leader always becomes the dominant firm. Under moderate uncertainty, preemption forces the leader to invest so early it cannot afford large capacity — potentially ending up smaller than the follower. Directly models the joint timing/scale decision facing hyperscalers. *Features: real options, duopoly, demand uncertainty, endogenous capacity choice, preemption.*

**Aguerrevere, Felipe L. (2003), "Equilibrium Investment Strategies and Output Price Behavior: A Real-Options Approach," *Review of Financial Studies*, 16(4), 1239–1272.** Studies incremental capacity investment with **time-to-build** and operating flexibility in oligopoly. The central finding reverses standard intuition: increased uncertainty may **encourage** capacity expansion because operating flexibility (the ability to idle capacity) makes extra capacity more valuable as a call option on future demand. Firms rationally build excess capacity even with underutilized existing infrastructure — matching hyperscaler behavior of building ahead of confirmed AI demand. *Features: real options, oligopoly, demand uncertainty, time-to-build, operating flexibility, incremental capacity.* Combines 4 of the 6 target features.

**Weeds, Helen (2002), "Strategic Delay in a Real Options Model of R&D Competition," *Review of Economic Studies*, 69(3), 729–747.** Studies a duopoly where firms hold options to invest in R&D with uncertain outcomes. The interaction of option value and preemption can produce either early investment (preemption equilibrium) or excessive delay (war of attrition), depending on relative first- and second-mover advantages. When first-mover advantage in AI capabilities is large, the model predicts preemption; when learning from others' mistakes dominates, firms delay. *Features: real options, duopoly, demand uncertainty, R&D investment, preemption vs. war of attrition.*

**Bouis, Romain, Kuno J.M. Huisman, and Peter M. Kort (2009), "Investment in Oligopoly Under Uncertainty: The Accordion Effect," *International Journal of Industrial Organization*, 27(2), 320–331.** One of the few models extending real options games to **N ≥ 3 firms**, directly matching the 3–4 hyperscaler market structure. Discovers an "accordion effect" where demand shocks change the wedge between successive investors' thresholds non-uniformly. Two equilibrium types emerge: sequential investment at distinct times, or simultaneous investment by the first two firms followed by delayed third-firm entry. The latter pattern — Microsoft and Google investing simultaneously, Amazon and Meta following — may characterize the current AI compute race. *Features: real options, N-firm oligopoly, demand uncertainty, preemption.*

**Kulatilaka, Nalin and Enrico C. Perotti (1998), "Strategic Growth Options," *Management Science*, 44(8), 1021–1031.** Shows that when strategic commitment is valuable, increased uncertainty can *encourage* investment in growth options — uncertainty becomes opportunity rather than risk. Building GPU capacity creates a growth option: the strategic ability to serve AI workloads competitors cannot. This provides the theoretical foundation for "build it and they will come." *Features: real options, Cournot competition, demand uncertainty, growth options.*

**Back, Kerry and Dirk Paulsen (2009), "Open-Loop Equilibria and Perfect Competition in Option Exercise Games," *Review of Financial Studies*, 22(11), 4531–4552.** An important methodological correction showing that Grenadier (2002)'s investment boundaries are open-loop equilibria. Under closed-loop strategies (firms observe rivals' actions — which hyperscalers can), preemption incentives are even stronger, and the competitive outcome approaches the zero-option-value benchmark. Implies the AI capacity race may be even more aggressive than Grenadier's framework predicts. *Features: real options, oligopoly, preemption, open-loop vs. closed-loop.*

**Baldursson, Fridrik M. (1998), "Irreversible Investment Under Uncertainty in Oligopoly," *Journal of Economic Dynamics and Control*, 22(4), 627–644.** Studies continuous (upward-only) capacity adjustment in oligopoly with heterogeneous firms. Shows that if one firm has an early capacity lead, this dominance persists for extended periods. Relevant to persistent asymmetries among hyperscalers. *Features: real options, oligopoly, demand uncertainty, heterogeneous firms.*

Additional foundational references include **Fudenberg, Drew and Jean Tirole (1985), "Preemption and Rent Equalization in the Adoption of New Technology," *Review of Economic Studies*, 52(3), 383–401** (the game-theoretic foundation for all preemption models); **Aguerrevere, Felipe L. (2009), "Real Options, Product Market Competition, and Asset Returns," *Journal of Finance*, 64(2), 957–983** (links strategic capacity investment to asset pricing); and the book **Smit, Han T.J. and Lenos Trigeorgis (2004), *Strategic Investment: Real Options and Games*, Princeton University Press** (the comprehensive synthesis of real options and game theory).

## 3. Real options with default and financial distress risk

This cluster addresses the critical feature missing from most strategic investment models: the possibility that aggressive capacity investment financed with debt leads to bankruptcy. For AI infrastructure — where CoreWeave and others carry substantial leverage, and even cash-rich hyperscalers face opportunity costs — these models are essential.

**Kumar, Praveen and Vijay Yerramilli (2018), "Optimal Capital Structure and Investment with Real Options and Endogenous Debt Costs," *Review of Financial Studies*, 31(9), 3452–3490.** Perhaps the **most directly applicable paper for modeling AI capacity with default risk**. Firms jointly optimize financial leverage and irreversible capacity investment, with higher capacity increasing operating leverage and default probability but generating tax shields. The key finding: **financial leverage and capacity are substitutes** — firms building more capacity should use less debt. Predicts that higher uncertainty about AI demand reduces both optimal capacity and leverage simultaneously. *Features: real options, default risk (endogenous), demand uncertainty, capacity choice.*

**Hackbarth, Dirk, Richmond Mathews, and David Robinson (2014), "Capital Structure, Product Market Dynamics, and the Boundaries of the Firm," *Management Science*, 60(12), 2971–2993.** The **highest-feature-count paper identified** in the default risk cluster, combining real options, endogenous default, product market competition, and demand uncertainty in a unified framework. Studies how capital structure affects competitive investment dynamics. Directly maps to the question of how highly leveraged AI infrastructure firms interact competitively in the oligopolistic cloud market. *Features: real options, default risk, product market competition, demand uncertainty.* Combines 4 of 6 features.

**Sundaresan, Suresh, Neng Wang, and Jinqiang Yang (2015), "Dynamic Investment, Capital Structure, and Debt Overhang," *Review of Corporate Finance Studies*, 4(1), 1–42.** A parsimonious closed-form model with two sequential growth options and endogenous default. When existing debt is sufficiently high, firms **risk-shift by prematurely exercising growth options** — they overinvest because equity captures the upside while debt bears the downside. The second growth option is less valuable than the first (decreasing returns). Maps to staged data center buildouts where initial investments are more valuable. *Features: real options (sequential), default risk, demand uncertainty, decreasing returns.*

**Bolton, Patrick, Hui Chen, and Neng Wang (2011), "A Unified Theory of Tobin's q, Corporate Investment, Financing, and Risk Management," *Journal of Finance*, 66(5), 1545–1578.** Models a financially constrained firm making investment and hedging decisions with precautionary cash management. Cash depletion triggers costly recapitalization or liquidation. The marginal value of cash varies with financial position, creating state-dependent investment. Directly models the tension where aggressive AI infrastructure spending depletes reserves, increasing vulnerability if returns lag. *Features: real options, financial distress (via cash depletion), demand uncertainty, risk management.*

**Bolton, Patrick, Neng Wang, and Jinqiang Yang (2019), "Investment under Uncertainty with Financial Constraints," *Journal of Economic Theory*, 184, 104912.** Extends real options to financially constrained firms, showing that investment thresholds depend on **cash holdings** — cash-rich firms invest sooner. Financial constraints amplify the impact of uncertainty on investment. Predicts that Apple (large cash reserves) can afford to wait on AI infrastructure while cash-constrained firms face dual uncertainty about demand and financing ability. *Features: real options, financial constraints/distress, demand uncertainty.*

**Hennessy, Christopher A. (2004), "Tobin's Q, Debt Overhang, and Investment," *Journal of Finance*, 59(4), 1717–1742.** Shows that debt overhang is more severe for **long-lived assets** — a crucial insight given data centers' 15–20 year lifespans. Debt-financed AI infrastructure investments are particularly prone to underinvestment relative to first-best. *Features: real options, default risk, demand uncertainty, asset durability.*

**Hackbarth, Dirk and David C. Mauer (2012), "Optimal Priority Structure, Capital Structure, and Investment," *Review of Financial Studies*, 25(3), 747–796.** Shows that jointly optimal capital and priority structures can virtually eliminate debt overhang distortions on growth option exercise. Relevant to designing optimal debt tranches for data center project finance. *Features: real options, default risk, demand uncertainty, debt priority structure.*

**Childs, Paul D., David C. Mauer, and Steven H. Ott (2005), "Interactions of Corporate Financing and Investment Decisions: The Effects of Agency Conflicts," *Journal of Financial Economics*, 76(3), 667–690** and **Mauer, David C. and Alexander J. Triantis (1994), "Interactions of Corporate Financing and Investment Decisions: A Dynamic Framework," *Journal of Finance*, 49(4), 1253–1277** both model how debt financing creates investment distortions (risk-shifting → overinvestment; debt overhang → underinvestment) in real options frameworks with endogenous default. Childs et al. show that shorter debt maturity mitigates underinvestment, relevant to debt structure choices by AI infrastructure firms. *Features: real options, default risk, demand uncertainty.*

**Miao, Jianjun (2005), "Optimal Capital Structure and Industry Dynamics," *Journal of Finance*, 60(6), 2621–2659.** An industry equilibrium model with heterogeneous firms making entry, exit, investment, and financing decisions under idiosyncratic and aggregate shocks — one of the few papers combining **default risk with competitive dynamics**. Produces predictions about leverage ratios, credit spreads, and default rates. *Features: real options, default risk, competitive industry dynamics, demand uncertainty.*

**Arnold, Marc, Alexander F. Wagner, and Ramona Westermann (2013), "Growth Options, Macroeconomic Conditions, and the Cross Section of Credit Risk," *Journal of Financial Economics*, 107(2), 350–385.** Firms whose value is mostly in growth options face higher credit risk during downturns — the option value collapses, leaving debt poorly covered. Directly predicts that AI infrastructure firms, whose valuations depend heavily on unrealized AI demand, are particularly vulnerable to credit risk spikes if the AI narrative falters. *Features: real options, default risk, macroeconomic uncertainty.*

**Aretz, Kevin and Peter F. Pope (2018), "Real Options of the Firm, Capacity Overhang, and the Cross-Section of Stock Returns," *Journal of Finance*, 73(3), 1363–1415.** Introduces "capacity overhang" as a state variable — excess capacity relative to demand increases systematic risk. Directly maps to the scenario where AI data center capacity exceeds realized demand, predicting higher risk premiums and cost of capital for over-built firms. *Features: real options, capacity overhang, demand uncertainty, asset pricing.*

## 4. Demand uncertainty with adoption timing, regime shifts, and technology diffusion

The distinctive feature of AI demand uncertainty is not just volatility but **uncertain timing of adoption inflections** — whether and when enterprise AI adoption reaches escape velocity.

**Guo, Xin, Jianjun Miao, and Erwan Morellec (2005), "Irreversible Investment with Regime Shifts," *Journal of Economic Theory*, 122(1), 37–59.** Allows demand growth rate and volatility to shift between regimes at random Markov-switching times. Investment is typically small but features occasional **large jumps** when a favorable regime arrives. Different investment thresholds apply in different regimes. This directly models the possibility that AI enterprise adoption transitions from slow-growth to rapid-adoption at an unknown time. Firms should have different hurdle rates depending on which regime they believe prevails — and invest in large lumps when a regime switch is detected. *Features: real options, demand uncertainty with regime switching, capacity choice.*

**Rob, Rafael and Nikolaos Vettas (2003), "Foreign Direct Investment and Exports with Growing Demand," *Review of Economic Studies*, 70(3), 629–648.** Models entry into a market with uncertain demand growth where firms can choose between a flexible high-marginal-cost option (exporting) and irreversible low-marginal-cost capacity (FDI). Firms optimally combine both: flexible production initially, committing to irreversible capacity as demand is confirmed. This maps precisely to AI compute: firms can rent cloud GPU instances (flexible, expensive) or build dedicated data centers (irreversible, cheaper per unit). Rational firms start flexible and gradually commit. *Features: real options, uncertain demand growth, two production modes.*

**Bloom, Nicholas (2009), "The Impact of Uncertainty Shocks," *Econometrica*, 77(3), 623–685.** A structural model with time-varying volatility shocks and both convex and non-convex adjustment costs. Uncertainty shocks cause firms to temporarily "pause" investment, generating rapid drops and rebounds. Explains the "wait and see" dynamic when AI sentiment shifts — GPU orders can swing dramatically with uncertainty about adoption timelines. *Features: real options, second-moment demand shocks, lumpy investment, general equilibrium.*

**Bloom, Nicholas, Stephen Bond, and John Van Reenen (2007), "Uncertainty and Investment Dynamics," *Review of Economic Studies*, 74(2), 391–415.** Provides **empirical validation** of real options theory using firm-level panel data, confirming that firms facing higher uncertainty respond more weakly to demand shocks. Essential for calibrating any real options model of AI compute investment.

**Jovanovic, Boyan and Peter L. Rousseau (2005), "General Purpose Technologies," in *Handbook of Economic Growth*, Vol. 1B, Elsevier, 1181–1224.** Documents that GPT adoption features S-curve diffusion taking **decades** (electricity: ~35 years; IT: 50+ years), initial productivity slowdowns, and massive resource reallocation. If AI follows this pattern, current capacity buildout plans may be premature by years or decades — a structural argument for overinvestment risk. *Features: technology diffusion S-curves, adoption timing uncertainty, GPT framework.*

## 5. R&D investment with diminishing returns and AI scaling laws

The economics of AI training exhibits clear power-law (log-linear) relationships between compute investment and model capability — the "scaling laws" documented by Kaplan et al. (2020) and Hoffmann et al. (2022). The growth theory literature has studied analogous structures for decades.

**Bloom, Nicholas, Charles I. Jones, John Van Reenen, and Michael Webb (2020), "Are Ideas Getting Harder to Find?," *American Economic Review*, 110(4), 1104–1144.** Documents across domains — including semiconductors, agriculture, and firm-level R&D — that **research productivity is declining sharply** while research effort rises. Sustaining Moore's Law required **18× more researchers** between 1970 and 2020. The idea production function Ȧ = δ·S^λ·A^φ with φ < 1 implies strongly diminishing returns. This is the closest economic analog to AI neural scaling laws: each capability increment requires exponentially more compute. *Features: diminishing returns to R&D (central), scaling laws.*

**Jones, Charles I. (1995), "R&D-Based Models of Economic Growth," *Journal of Political Economy*, 103(4), 759–784.** Generalizes endogenous growth to allow diminishing returns in idea production (φ < 1). The core insight — doubling R&D effort does not double growth — maps directly to AI scaling laws' sub-linear relationship between compute and capability. Also models the labor allocation between researchers (H_A) and workers (H_Y), paralleling GPU allocation between training and inference. *Features: diminishing returns to R&D, resource allocation between R&D and production.*

**Aghion, Philippe and Peter Howitt (1992), "A Model of Growth Through Creative Destruction," *Econometrica*, 60(2), 323–351.** The Schumpeterian quality-ladder model where innovations arrive stochastically via a Poisson process, each creating temporary monopoly rents while rendering predecessors obsolete. Each new frontier AI model (GPT-3 → GPT-4 → GPT-5) is a quality "step" that partially obsoletes predecessors — creating strong investment incentives but also potential overinvestment through creative destruction. *Features: stochastic innovation, creative destruction, strategic competition, step-function improvements.*

**Grossman, Gene M. and Elhanan Helpman (1991), "Quality Ladders in the Theory of Growth," *Review of Economic Studies*, 58(1), 43–61.** Models endogenous allocation between R&D (quality improvements) and manufacturing (serving current demand) with quality-ladder dynamics. Quality steps map to discrete AI model generations; the endogenous R&D vs. manufacturing split maps to the training vs. inference compute allocation. *Features: step-function improvements, two-sided investment (R&D vs. manufacturing), strategic R&D competition.*

**Kortum, Samuel S. (1997), "Research, Patenting, and Technological Change," *Econometrica*, 65(6), 1389–1419.** Models research as random search where the probability of beating the frontier falls as the frontier advances — the "fishing out" effect generates diminishing returns naturally. Directly analogous to the hypothesis that AI scaling is approaching fundamental limits. *Features: diminishing returns to research, stochastic innovation.*

## 6. Two-sided investment: R&D versus production allocation

The AI compute allocation problem — how to split GPU capacity between training (R&D) and inference (production/revenue) — has direct analogs in the endogenous growth literature on exploration vs. exploitation.

**Akcigit, Ufuk and William R. Kerr (2018), "Growth Through Heterogeneous Innovations," *Journal of Political Economy*, 126(4), 1374–1443.** The **most relevant paper for the two-sided allocation problem**. Multi-product firms simultaneously invest in "internal" (exploitation) R&D to improve existing products and "external" (exploration) R&D to acquire new capabilities. Internal innovation scales more strongly with firm size than external innovation. Maps directly to AI firms allocating between inference optimization/fine-tuning (exploitation, scales with customer base) and training next-generation models (exploration, scales with ambition for capability jumps). *Features: two-sided investment (exploration vs. exploitation), strategic competition, diminishing returns, firm heterogeneity.*

**Klette, Tor Jakob and Samuel Kortum (2004), "Innovating Firms and Aggregate Innovation," *Journal of Political Economy*, 112(5), 986–1018.** Firms are collections of production units, each subject to creative destruction. R&D adds new product lines while existing lines fund operations and face destruction by competitors. Maps to AI firms managing portfolios of deployed models (inference revenue) while investing in next-generation capabilities, with competitor innovation rendering existing models obsolete. *Features: two-sided investment, strategic competition, creative destruction, dynamic firm lifecycle.*

**Berk, Jonathan B., Richard C. Green, and Vasant Naik (1999), "Optimal Investment, Growth Options, and Security Returns," *Journal of Finance*, 54(5), 1553–1607.** Dynamic model where firm value consists of assets-in-place (current revenue) and growth options (future investment opportunities). The mix determines systematic risk and expected returns. AI firms' valuations reflect both current inference revenue and option value of future model generations — the growth-option-intensive composition makes them inherently riskier. *Features: real options, two-sided value (assets vs. growth options), demand uncertainty.*

**Lentz, Rasmus and Dale T. Mortensen (2008), "An Empirical Model of Growth Through Product Innovation," *Econometrica*, 76(6), 1317–1373.** Structural estimation of the Klette-Kortum framework using Danish firm data. Heterogeneity in R&D productivity drives firm growth differences. Could be adapted to quantify returns to AI training investment and cross-lab differences in compute efficiency. *Features: two-sided investment, creative destruction, diminishing returns, firm heterogeneity.*

## 7. Papers combining the most features: closest to a unified AI compute model

A handful of papers combine four of the six target features, making them the strongest starting points for a unified model:

**Della Seta, Marco, Sebastian Gryglewicz, and Peter M. Kort (2012), "Optimal Investment in Learning-Curve Technologies," *Journal of Economic Dynamics and Control*, 36(10), 1462–1476.** Combines real options with learning curves where production costs decline with cumulative output. The learning curve generates scale incentives for early investment even under uncertainty. For AI, early compute investment accumulates learning (in hardware deployment, model training, data curation), creating durable cost advantages. *Features: real options, demand uncertainty, diminishing returns/learning, two-sided dynamics.* 4 of 6 features.

**Aguerrevere (2003)** and **Huisman and Kort (2015)**, discussed above, each combine real options, oligopoly, demand uncertainty, and capacity choice — 4 of 6 features. Neither includes default risk or two-sided investment.

**Hackbarth, Mathews, and Robinson (2014)** combines real options, default risk, product market competition, and demand uncertainty — 4 of 6 features — but lacks capacity choice and diminishing returns.

**Miao (2005)** combines real options, default risk, industry competition, and demand uncertainty — 4 features — but treats entry/exit rather than continuous capacity choice.

## Conclusion: the literature gap and how to fill it

The academic literature provides exceptionally strong building blocks for modeling AI compute infrastructure investment, but no published paper integrates all six ingredients simultaneously. Two critical gaps stand out. First, **default risk and oligopoly competition have never been combined with capacity choice** in a real options framework — papers either model default without competition (Kumar and Yerramilli, Sundaresan et al.) or competition without default (Grenadier, Huisman and Kort). Second, the **two-sided allocation between R&D and production** (training vs. inference compute) has been studied in growth theory but never embedded in a real options game with financial constraints.

A constructive path to a unified model would start with **Huisman and Kort (2015)** as the strategic backbone (duopoly timing and capacity choice under uncertainty), layer in **endogenous default** following Kumar and Yerramilli (2018) or Hackbarth, Mathews, and Robinson (2014), add **regime-switching demand** from Guo, Miao, and Morellec (2005) to capture adoption timing uncertainty, and incorporate **diminishing returns** via a concave production function calibrated to empirical AI scaling laws (Bloom et al. 2020). The two-sided allocation could follow Akcigit and Kerr (2018)'s exploration-exploitation structure. Such a model — applied to the $300B+ annual AI infrastructure investment cycle among 3–4 hyperscalers — would be novel and timely. The building blocks are mature; the synthesis is the contribution.
